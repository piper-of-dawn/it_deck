When entrepreneurs make their investment decisions, they do not know whether they will end up with a high-productivity good or a low-productivity good.

The \(\theta\) associated with an investment project is discovered only after the investment is sunk. All that entrepreneurs know ex ante is that \(\theta\) is distributed uniformly over the range \([0, h]\)

The process of discovering the underlying cost structure of the economy, which is intrinsically uncertain, contributes a stochastic dimension to what a country will produce and therefore how rich it will be.

Let \(\theta^{max}\) represent the productivity of the most sophisticated product of the economy.

Every investor has two choices, either to emulate the most sophisticated product or to innovate their own product.

Emulators operate at a fraction \(\alpha\) of the incumbent in productivity, with \(0 \leq \alpha \leq 1\) .

Hence each inventor now faces an uniformly distributed productivity from \(\alpha \theta_{max}\) to \(h\)

Entrepreneurship and Sophistication

Suppose there are \(m\) entrepreneurs in an economy each trying to innovate products the sophistication of which is uniformly distributed over \([0,h]\)

We will find the expected value of the maximum sophistication that will be achieved in this economy with \(m\) entrepreneurs. It is given as:

$$ E[\theta^\max] = {{hm} \over {m+1}} $$

Assume that there are \(m\) entrepreneurs in the economy, each independentally trying to innovate a product, the productivity \(\theta\) of is uniformally distributed over the interval \([0,h]\)

\(\{\theta_1 \ldots \theta_m\}\) is a sequence of IID uniform random variables.

We are interested in the maximum productivity an economy will expect given there are \(m\) entrepreneurs i,i.e \(\theta^{\max}=E[\max\{\theta_1 \ldots \theta_m\}]\) is a sequence of IID uniform random variables.

$$P(\theta \leq \theta^{\max}) = P(\theta_1 \leq \theta^{\max}, ..., \theta_m \leq \theta^{\max}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(\theta_i \leq \theta^{\max}) = [F_{\theta^{\max}}(\theta^{\max})]^m$$

where \([F_{\theta^{\max}}(\theta^{\max})]^m\) is the CDF of \(\theta^{\max}\)

Derivation of \(E[\theta^\max]\) continued

The probability that the investor \(i\) will innovate is:

$$\text{prob}\left(\theta_{i} \geq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$ = \int_{\alpha \theta^{\max}}^h {1 \over h} d\theta $$
$$ = 1-\frac{\alpha E[\theta^{\max }]}{h}$$
$$ = 1- {\alpha m \over {m+1}}$$

The expected profit if investor innovates is given a exogenously determined world price \(p\)

$$E\left(\pi \mid \theta_{i} \geq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p \left[h+\alpha E\left(\theta^{\max }\right)\right]$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\frac{\alpha m}{m+1}\right]$$

The probability that the entrepreneur \(i\) will copy the most sophisticated product is:

$$\text{prob}\left(\theta_{i} \leq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$ = \int_0^{\alpha \theta^{\max}}{1 \over h} d\theta $$
$$ = \frac{\alpha E[\theta^{\max }]}{h}$$
$$ = {\alpha m \over {m+1}}$$

The expected profit if entrepreneur copies the most sophisticated product, given a exogenously determined world price \(p\)

$$E\left(\pi \mid \theta_{i} \leq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p \left[0+\alpha E\left(\theta^{\max }\right)\right]$$
$$= p h\left[\frac{\alpha m}{m+1}\right]$$

Now, entrepreneurs have two options whether to copy or to innovate we can calculate the expected profitability of the modern sector as:

$$ {(1-{{\alpha m} \over {m+1} })}{{1 \over 2}ph (1+ {{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} )} $$
$$+ {{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} \ ({ph ({{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} ))}$$

This gives expected profitability of the modern sector as:

$$ \frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\left(\frac{\alpha m}{m+1}\right)^{2}\right] $$

This gives expected profitability of the modern sector is independent of \(m\) and only dependent upon world price \(p\) and skill level \(h\).

Let \(m^*\) represent the long run value of \(m\). And this gives the expected profitability of modern sector as:

$$ r\left(p, h, m^{*}\right)=E(\pi)_{L R}=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\left(\frac{\alpha m^{*}}{m^{*}+1}\right)^{2}\right] $$

We have already seen the investor incurs a sunk investment of \(bw^*\) where \(w^*\) represents the long run value of \(w\). Therefore, the zero long-run profit condition would be:

$$ r\left(p, h, m^{*}\right)=E(\pi)_{L R}=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\left(\frac{\alpha m^{*}}{m^{*}+1}\right)^{2}\right] $$

where \(\rho\) represents the discount rate.

We will call this relationship between \(w\) and \(m\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(ZZ\) schedule.

We will now introduce the labour market equilibrium:

Wages are determined by the where the demand of labour equals its fixed supply: \(L\).

Demand for labour:

Demand in modern sector + Demand in traditional sector

Demand in modern sector is a function of number of investors \(m^*\) and we will call it \(bm^*\)

Demand in traditional sector is a function of wage rate \(w^*\) and we will call it \(g(w^*)\)

Therefore labour market equilibrium is given as:

$$ bm^* + g(w^*) = L $$

We will call this \(LL\) relation.

We have a linear \(ZZ\) relation as: $$ r\left(p, h, m^{*}\right)=E(\pi)_{L R}=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\left(\frac{\alpha m^{*}}{m^{*}+1}\right)^{2}\right] $$

We have a linear \(LL\) relation as: $$ bm^* + g(w^*) = L $$

We can show this on a \(m-w\) plane.

Suppose, for example, that we start at a level of m which falls short of m^*. Consider how, these dynamics influence the decision to enter. The rise in m implies that productivity will be higher in the future than it is today, and is a force that will induce delay in the decision to invest in the modern sector ceteris paribus.

The rise in w, on the other hand, implies that investment will be more costly in the future than it is today, and is a factor that will precipitate investment. Given the relative slopes we have assumed, the second factor outweighs the first, wages increase faster than the rate at which productivity benefits come investors would rather invest today than wait.

On the transition to the long-run equilibrium, we know that m and w will both rise.

This shifts the \(LL\) schedule down since, at a given \(m\), Labor market equilibrium requires lower wages. Hence the impact of larger \(L\) is a lower \(w\).

However, the lower wage induces more firms to enter the modern sector and engage in cost discovery, which in turn pulls wages up.

This brings in more investors \(m\) in the economy which pulls up the wages and equilibrium is restored.

We have seen then productivity in an economy is dependent upon \(\theta_{\max}\).

The authors construct a empirical proxy called \(EXPY\) to measure sophistication or \(\theta_{\max}\) of the country.

Let countries be indexed by \(j\) and goods by \(l\). Therefore the total exports of the country are given as: $$ X_j = \sum x_{jl} $$

Let per capita GDP be denoted by \(Y_j\). Therefore we can measure productivity associated with product \(k\) as: $$ P R O D Y_{k}=\sum_{j} \frac{\left(x_{j k} / X_{j}\right)}{\sum_{j}\left(x_{j k} / X_{j}\right)} Y_{j} $$

The numerator \(x_{j k} / X_{j}\) is the value-share of the commodity in the country's overall export basket and the denominator \(\sum_j x_{j k} / X_{j}\), aggregates the value-shares across all countries exporting the good. We have weighted this ratio by the per-capita income of the country assuming that richer countries export more sophisticated products.

Now we weight the index \(PRODY\) by the value share of the commodity in country's total exports to get: $$ E X P Y_{i}=\sum_{l}\left(\frac{x_{i l}}{X_{i}}\right) P R O D Y_{l} $$
