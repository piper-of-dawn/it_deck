What you Export Matters

When local cost discovery generates knowledge spillovers, specialization patterns become partly indeterminate and the mix of goods that a country produces may have important implications for economic growth.

When entrepreneurs make their investment decisions, they do not know whether they will end up with a high-productivity good or a low-productivity good.

The \(\theta\) associated with an investment project is discovered only after the investment is sunk. All that entrepreneurs know ex ante is that \(\theta\) is distributed uniformly over the range \([0, h]\)

The process of discovering the underlying cost structure of the economy, which is intrinsically uncertain, contributes a stochastic dimension to what a country will produce and therefore how rich it will be.

Let \(\theta^{max}\) represent the productivity of the most sophisticated product of the economy.

Every investor has two choices, either to emulate the most sophisticated product or to innovate their own product.

Emulators operate at a fraction \(\alpha\) of the incumbent in productivity, with \(0 \leq \alpha \leq 1\) .

Hence each inventor now faces an uniformly distributed productivity from \(\alpha \theta_{max}\) to \(h\)

Entrepreneurship and Sophistication

Suppose there are \(m\) entrepreneurs in an economy each trying to innovate products the sophistication of which is uniformly distributed over \([0,h]\)

We will find the expected value of the maximum sophistication that will be achieved in this economy with \(m\) entrepreneurs. It is given as:

$$ E[\theta^\max] = {hm} \over {m+1} $$

Assume that there are \(m\) entrepreneurs in the economy, each independentally trying to innovate a product, the productivity \(\theta\) of is uniformally distributed over the interval \([0,h]\)

\(\{\theta_1 \ldots \theta_m\}\) is a sequence of IID uniform random variables.

We are interested in the maximum productivity an economy will expect given there are \(m\) entrepreneurs i,i.e \(\theta^{\max}=E[\max\{\theta_1 \ldots \theta_m\}]\) is a sequence of IID uniform random variables.

$$P(\theta \leq \theta^{\max}) = P(\theta_1 \leq \theta^{\max}, ..., \theta_m \leq \theta^{\max}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(\theta_i \leq \theta^{\max}) = [F_{\theta^{\max}}(\theta^{\max})]^m$$

where \([F_{\theta^{\max}}(\theta^{\max})]^m\) is the CDF of \(\theta^{\max}\)

Derivation of \(E[\theta^\max]\) continued

The probability that the investor \(i\) will innovate is:

$$\text{prob}\left(\theta_{i} \geq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$ = \int_{\alpha \theta^{\max}}^h {1 \over h} d\theta $$
$$ = 1-\frac{\alpha E[\theta^{\max }]}{h}$$
$$ = 1- {\alpha m \over {m+1}}$$

The expected profit if investor innovates is given a exogenously determined world price \(p\)

$$E\left(\pi \mid \theta_{i} \geq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p \left[h+\alpha E\left(\theta^{\max }\right)\right]$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[1+\frac{\alpha m}{m+1}\right]$$

The probability that the entrepreneur \(i\) will copy the most sophisticated product is:

$$\text{prob}\left(\theta_{i} \leq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$ = \int_0^{\alpha \theta^{\max}}{1 \over h} d\theta $$
$$ = \frac{\alpha E[\theta^{\max }]}{h}$$
$$ = {\alpha m \over {m+1}}$$

The expected profit if entrepreneur copies the most sophisticated product, given a exogenously determined world price \(p\)

$$E\left(\pi \mid \theta_{i} \leq \alpha \theta^{\max }\right)$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p \left[0+\alpha E\left(\theta^{\max }\right)\right]$$
$$=\frac{1}{2} p h\left[\frac{\alpha m}{m+1}\right]$$

Now, entrepreneurs have two options whether to copy or to innovate we can calculate the expected profitability of the modern sector as:

$$ {(1-{{\alpha m} \over {m+1} })}{{1 \over 2}ph (1+ {{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} )} $$
$$+ {{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} \ ({{1 \over 2}ph ({{\alpha m} \over {m+1}} ))}$$

This gives expected profitability of the modern sector as:

$$ {1 \over 2} ph $$

This gives expected profitability of the modern sector is independent of \(m\) and only dependent upon world price \(p\) and skill level \(h\).
